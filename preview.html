Preface

This book was born on the Erasmus bridge in Rotterdam, on a cold winter night when I completely surrendered—when I finally let go of the illusion that I could control life's outcomes.

As an economist studying Small Island Developing States, I've spent years analyzing how islands like my home of Aruba navigate their unique vulnerabilities. My work has always been data-driven, theoretical, distant. I believed in control through analysis, in managing risk through careful calculation. When I returned to Aruba with my master's degree in development economics, I had checked all the right boxes—the respectable job, the house, the car. Yet despite this material success, I felt profoundly empty, as if I'd built an impressive shell around nothing of substance.

It wasn't until my family faced our own crisis—when our unborn son was diagnosed with congenital diaphragmatic hernia—that I truly began to understand the human dimensions of resilience. More than that, this journey forced me to confront my own attempts to negotiate with vulnerability, to maintain control even in surrender, to build protective boundaries that ultimately had to shatter for transformation to occur.

In the pages that follow, I share how this journey transformed my understanding of what it means to be resilient. I explore how the queen conch, a marine mollusk native to Caribbean waters, offers a powerful metaphor: its remarkable shell, perfected over millennia to protect against predators, now leaves it defenseless against warming oceans and industrial fishing. The protective shell that once guaranteed survival now contributes to its vulnerability: too heavy to escape harvest, too specialized to adapt quickly, too visible to hide.

This book weaves together multiple journeys: my family's path through medical crisis and my research into economic resilience in island nations.

While based on real experiences, I've compressed some timelines and created composite characters for certain medical staff to respect their privacy while preserving the essential truths of our journey. The names of my immediate family members are real and used with their permission.

My hope is that by exploring resilience through intimate personal experience and professional research, readers will gain a richer understanding of how we might navigate an increasingly uncertain world.

I owe profound gratitude to God for guiding us through our darkest moments when faith became the only solid ground beneath our feet. To my wife, Lay Hing, whose unwavering strength and faith showed me what I had been missing. To our children, who taught me more about resilience than any academic paper ever could. To the medical teams in Rotterdam whose expertise saved our son's life. And to the communities of both Aruba and Rotterdam, whose different approaches to resilience enriched my understanding.

As you read this book, I invite you to consider how these resilience patterns manifest in your own life and work. In business, the same paradoxes emerge: companies build competitive advantages through specialization, only to find those very strengths become liabilities when markets shift. Organizations develop efficient processes that serve them well until disruption demands flexibility they can no longer access. We create protective structures—whether corporate strategies, or personal boundaries—that promise safety but may prevent the very transformation necessary for survival.

Whether you're leading a team through uncertainty, guiding a company through transformation, navigating your own career in changing times, or simply trying to maintain control in an uncontrollable world, the queen conch's lesson remains relevant: our shells protect us, but they also define our limits. Understanding when to strengthen our defenses and when to risk complete vulnerability for growth—when to hold tight and when to surrender—may be the most critical skill in our interconnected, rapidly changing world.

Our shells protect us, but they also define our limits. True resilience lies in knowing when to abandon them.

Rendell de Kort
Savaneta, Aruba
November 2025

Chapter 1: The Conch Paradox

This book is about a dangerous misconception that shapes how we respond to every threat we face—from medical emergencies to economic crises to climate change. We believe that building stronger defenses makes us safer. We assume that specialized protection reduces vulnerability. We're wrong.

The truth is counterintuitive: the adaptations that make us most resilient to familiar threats often leave us most vulnerable to novel ones. The shell that protects also constrains. The expertise that saves us in one crisis may blind us to the next. What we think of as our greatest strengths are often our most profound weaknesses.

I discovered this paradox on the Erasmus Bridge in Rotterdam, during the darkest night of my family's life, when the decision to save my son's life required surrendering control of it entirely.

The bridge loomed before me in the winter darkness, its asymmetrical silhouette cutting through the night sky like a question mark against the stars. I was dressed absurdly for the freezing Dutch winter—wearing shorts and borrowed women's gloves from my wife—running to escape the overwhelming weight of the decision we had just made.

We had arrived in Rotterdam just 48 hours earlier, our lives upended by a diagnosis that would test everything I thought I knew about resilience—both as an economist studying island vulnerabilities and as a father trying to protect his family. The meeting with the surgeon had lasted exactly one hour. In that time, we'd had to choose: undergo an experimental operation to stimulate lung growth but risk premature birth, or trust that our son would survive on his own strength.

Each step up the bridge's incline sent sharp pains through my legs. I was grossly out of shape, wearing an overly tight shirt from my glory days and those ridiculous feminine gloves that barely kept my fingers from going numb. But I couldn't stop—I had to reach the other side. If I can conquer this bridge, I thought desperately, my unborn son can conquer his medical obstacle.

As I reached the apex and began the merciful downhill slope, my eyes automatically sought the building where I had lived as a student—the first place where I had tasted real freedom twenty years earlier. But when I spotted the familiar structure, I stopped breathing entirely.

The entire façade was covered with a massive mural I had never seen before—a mother holding her newborn child. The image was rendered in soft, grayish tones that seemed to glow against the building's surface, dominated by a full moon that hung like a halo behind the mother's head. She was gazing down at her child with an expression of pure love and protection, holding the infant close to her chest in the most natural, tender embrace imaginable.

The sight hit me like a physical blow. Here I was, fighting for my son's chance to live, staring at the exact image of what I prayed we would someday experience. The cruel beauty of the timing, the impossible coincidence of this mural appearing on this exact building during our darkest hour, felt like either divine intervention or cosmic mockery.

That's when I broke. I stood there sobbing in the Dutch winter night, my tears freezing on my cheeks almost as soon as they fell. In that moment of unexpected synchronicity, standing before the mural of a mother holding her child, I felt something profound: sometimes the most protective decision is the one that feels like surrender.

As I walked back to the hotel, my mind sought refuge in the familiar territory of my research. Six months earlier, before any of this began, I'd been so proud of my PhD outline on economic resilience in Small Island Developing States. I'd chosen the queen conch as my central metaphor—its remarkable three-layered shell structure could withstand tremendous pressure, dissipate force, and contain damage. Materials scientists studied it. Engineers tried to replicate it. What better symbol for how small islands could build unbreakable defenses?

My supervisors had demolished it in our video call. "Using a metaphor is cute," my second supervisor had said with Dutch directness, "but why are we using a conch as an ideal for resilience? It's an endangered species. If you're looking for a resilient creature, shouldn't we be looking at a cockroach instead?"

A cockroach instead of a conch. The words had stung for weeks. In that meeting, I'd salvaged what I could, agreeing to mention the conch only briefly in the introduction—"maybe the conch lives to fight another day?" I'd said, trying to save face. My supervisor had smirked.

But now, walking through the frozen Dutch night with my son's life hanging in the balance, I wondered if my supervisor had unknowingly given me the key to everything. The conch was endangered. Despite its perfect shell. Because of its perfect shell. The very specialization that had protected it for millennia now prevented it from adapting to warming oceans and industrial fishing methods.

"'Thank God,' Lay Hing said when I walked in. 'I was starting to worry.' As I recounted the bridge and the mural, she listened with understanding. The decision to forgo surgery had been made, but I'd needed to run myself to exhaustion and sob before a stranger's mural to truly accept it. She had found her peace in prayer while I'd searched for mine in the frozen Dutch night."

The pattern everywhere

Over the following months, as we navigated our son's medical crisis, I began to see this paradox everywhere I looked. Caribbean islands had spent decades perfecting their tourism economies, building a specialization so refined it could weather financial crises and hurricanes. Then COVID arrived—a threat no one had imagined—and that same specialization became a trap when global travel simply stopped.

The paradox haunted our daily life in Rotterdam. The sophisticated Dutch medical system that was saving our son's life was simultaneously creating dependencies we could never maintain back home. Every feeding tube, every monitor, every protocol that worked flawlessly in the Erasmus Medical Center would be impossible to replicate in Aruba. We were being saved and trapped by the same expertise.

Even in my marriage, the pattern emerged. Those analytical skills that let me navigate hospital bureaucracy and absorb medical research, that ability to compartmentalize emotion and focus on logistics—they had been invaluable during the crisis itself. But later, when Lay needed emotional connection during recovery, when she needed me to stop analyzing and just feel alongside her, those same skills became walls between us. My greatest professional strengths had become personal weaknesses.

The more I looked, the more I saw it: specialized adaptation creating its own unique vulnerabilities when the unprecedented arrived.
