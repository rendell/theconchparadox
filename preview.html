Preface

This book was born on the Erasmus Bridge in Rotterdam, on a freezing winter night when I finally surrendered—when I let go of the illusion that I could control life's outcomes.

As an economist studying Small Island Developing States, I'd spent years analyzing how islands like my home of Aruba navigate vulnerability. My work was data-driven, theoretical, distant. I believed in control through analysis, in managing risk through careful calculation. Even after returning to Aruba with my master's degree, checking all the right boxes—the respectable job, the house, the car—I felt profoundly empty, as if I'd built an impressive shell around nothing of substance.

Then our unborn son was diagnosed with congenital diaphragmatic hernia, a life-threatening condition that would require surgery in Rotterdam. Suddenly, all my professional expertise about resilience meant nothing. I couldn't analyze or calculate my way out of this crisis.

What I discovered in those desperate months changed everything—not just about my family's survival, but about the fundamental nature of resilience itself.

The queen conch, a Caribbean mollusk, embodies this paradox perfectly. Its magnificent shell—with distinctive pink interior and protective spines—has evolved over millions of years into one of nature's most effective defenses. Yet today, despite this armor, the species faces extinction. The same specializations that made it resilient against traditional predators now make it vulnerable to warming oceans and changing ecosystems. Its perfect shell has become a constraint.

This pattern appears everywhere: in island economies specialized for tourism, in medical systems built for routine care, in marriages built on unspoken expectations, in careers optimized for stability. Our greatest strengths become our greatest vulnerabilities when circumstances change dramatically.

This book weaves together three journeys: my family's medical crisis, my research into economic resilience, and a personal transformation I never saw coming. While based on real experiences, some details have been adjusted to protect privacy and illuminate larger truths.

My hope is that by exploring resilience through both intimate experience and professional research, you'll recognize your own paradoxes—the protective shells you've built that may simultaneously create vulnerabilities, and the wisdom required to know when to strengthen defenses and when to risk transformation.

Rendell de Kort
Savaneta, Aruba
November 2025

Chapter 1: The Conch Paradox

This book is about a dangerous misconception that shapes how we respond to every threat we face—from medical emergencies to economic crises to climate change. We believe that building stronger defenses makes us safer. We assume that specialized protection reduces vulnerability.

We're wrong.

The truth is counterintuitive: the adaptations that make us most resilient to familiar threats often leave us most vulnerable to novel ones. The shell that protects also constrains. The expertise that saves us in one crisis may blind us to the next. What we think of as our greatest strengths are often our most profound weaknesses.

I discovered this paradox on the Erasmus Bridge in Rotterdam, during the darkest night of my family's life, when the decision to save my son's life required surrendering control of it entirely.

The bridge loomed before me in the winter darkness, its asymmetrical silhouette cutting through the night sky like a question mark against the stars. I was dressed absurdly for the freezing Dutch winter—wearing shorts and borrowed women's gloves—as I ran to escape the overwhelming weight of the decision my wife and I had just made.

Just forty-eight hours earlier, we had been under the Aruban sun when our unborn son was diagnosed with a life-threatening diaphragmatic hernia. Now we stood in Rotterdam, having fought through bureaucratic barriers and crossed an ocean for experimental surgery that might save him. The specialists had presented us with impossible choices: an invasive procedure that could help his lungs develop but carried its own grave risks, or trusting in his natural resilience and accepting the uncertainty.

We chose to let go. To trust his body's own adaptive capacity rather than impose our will through medical intervention.

With each stride across the bridge's span, I felt the dual forces of hope and terror pulling me in opposite directions. This massive structure, nicknamed "The Swan," had challenged me as a university student two decades before. Now it witnessed another turning point in my life.

As I reached the other side, my breath clouding in the frigid air, I caught sight of my old apartment building, now remarkably adorned with a mural of a mother holding her infant child. In that moment of unexpected synchronicity, standing before the mural of a mother holding her child, the weight I had been carrying seemed to lift. This wasn't an intellectual revelation, but a profound moment of surrender. Exhausted from trying to control the uncontrollable, I felt something telling me: It's going to be OK.

The Erasmus Bridge had witnessed my youthful ambitions as a student, and now it became the place where I released my burdens and fears.

The Paradox Revealed

What I didn't realize that night was that I was literally running across the perfect metaphor for what I'd spent years studying professionally: the resilience paradox.

The Erasmus Bridge itself embodies this paradox. Engineers designed it to work with Rotterdam's powerful forces—wind, water, ship traffic—rather than rigidly resisting them. The bridge moves, flexes, adapts. What does it mean that a structure which appears vulnerable—which deliberately yields to pressure—proves more durable than rigid strength?

Rotterdam understood this principle because it had no choice. The city was destroyed during World War II, rebuilt from rubble, and then had to continuously adapt to rising sea levels and changing economic realities. Unlike cities that could afford to preserve historic structures and resist change, Rotterdam became a laboratory for adaptive design.

Perhaps there's a strange gift in devastation: when you have nothing left to preserve, transformation becomes possible.

The same pattern appears in nature. The queen conch, a marine mollusk native to Caribbean waters, has survived virtually unchanged for millions of years. Its shell represents one of nature's most sophisticated protective structures: a three-tiered composite that dissipates force, contains cracks, and withstands tremendous pressure. This magnificent armor—with its distinctive pink interior and protective spines—made the conch nearly invincible against its traditional predators.

Yet today, despite this evolutionary masterpiece, the species faces extinction. Warming oceans, habitat degradation, and industrial fishing present entirely different challenges than ancient predators. The shell that protected against one type of threat offers no defense against these novel dangers.

Can perfection itself become a trap? Can the very adaptations that ensure survival in one era guarantee extinction in another?

The Resilience Industrial Complex

Somewhere along the way, "resilience" became the answer to everything.

Can't handle climate change? Build resilience. Economic crisis looming? Develop resilience capacity. Struggling with work-life balance? You need personal resilience. The word has been so thoroughly drained of meaning that it now means everything and nothing simultaneously.

Organizations hire Chief Resilience Officers. Consultants sell resilience frameworks. Universities offer resilience certificates. Government agencies fund resilience programs. Everyone is building resilience. No one can quite define what it means.

Here's what it usually means in practice: bounce back to normal as quickly as possible. Build higher seawalls. Develop faster recovery protocols. Add more redundancy to critical systems. These aren't wrong, exactly. But they all assume the future will look like the past, just with bigger waves.

This is the resilience industrial complex: a self-perpetuating system where experts profit from teaching organizations how to build better versions of what they already have. It's comfortable because it doesn't require questioning your fundamental approach. You can purchase resilience assessments, implement resilience frameworks, and measure resilience metrics—all without actually transforming anything about how you operate.

The conch has been perfecting its shell for millions of years. By any conventional measure of resilience, it succeeded brilliantly—right up until the moment the definition of success changed entirely.

The Pattern Everywhere

Once you see the paradox, you recognize it everywhere. Or perhaps, once you stop looking away, you finally see what was always there.

Island economies specialize in tourism because they lack the scale for diverse manufacturing. This specialization brings prosperity—until a pandemic shuts down international travel and exposes the vulnerability created by that very focus. The economic strategy that built wealth has simultaneously created fragility.

Medical systems optimize for efficiency and routine care, eliminating slack capacity and standardizing protocols. This works beautifully under normal conditions—until a novel virus arrives and the system's specialized efficiency becomes its greatest weakness. What looked like waste turns out to be essential for handling the unexpected.

Marriages develop comfortable patterns and unspoken expectations, creating stability through predictability. But when crisis hits, those same patterns can become rigid constraints that prevent the adaptation required for survival. What if the protective routines that make a relationship feel secure are exactly what prevent it from evolving when evolution becomes necessary?

Companies invest deeply in core competencies, building competitive advantages through specialization. Then the market shifts, and those same deep investments become sunk costs that prevent pivoting to new opportunities. The expertise that created success becomes the anchor that prevents transformation.

In each case, the adaptation that created resilience against familiar challenges has simultaneously created vulnerability to novel ones. The shell protects and constrains.

Perhaps strength and weakness aren't opposites at all. Perhaps they're two faces of the same choice.
